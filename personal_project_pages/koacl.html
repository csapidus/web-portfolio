Koa Wood IN-8 Nixie Clock

My first Nixie clock! As such, it uses a kit purchased from pvelectronics. The Nixie tubes are IN-8s, which are medium sized (about three inches) and moderately priced. Koa is a goldish brown hawaiian wood in high demand and short supply. Plexiglass makes up the front of the Koa box. Scott McDuff was of tremendous help in putting the box together, and was kind enough to teach me the basics of woodworking. I thoroughly enjoyed making this clock, and the experience no doubt spurred my interest in chronometry.

Glued + Clamped

Elmer's wood-glue was applied to the planks of wood. 30-45 minutes to dry, with clamps holding the planks together. Pressure is needed to ensure proper bonding.

Semi-Gloss Lacquer Top-Coat

Then apply the semi-gloss lacquer top-coat. We're applying it to achieve a certain sheen. Apply fairly generously, but not stupidly.

Disclaimer:

Koa is lovely but expensive, and there are many equally beautiful hardwoods out there for less. I purchased this block online, but I suggest saving a pretty penny by visiting a local lumber yard instead. Also, you'll be better able to judge the wood in-person.

Impress your friends with some cool facts about Koa

- Color: Gold to brown with dark bands
- Odor: None
- Origins: Hawaii (this particular variety)
- Common Uses: Musical instruments, bowls, furniture, etc.

Lacquer-Base + Return of the Sand[-ing] Sponge

The clear liquid on the left is a semi-gloss lacquer top-coat. The milky liquid on the right is a sanding sealer. The sanding sealer is applied first, and then left to dry for about twenty minutes. Once dry, a 220 grit sanding sponge sands off the insides and exterior.
